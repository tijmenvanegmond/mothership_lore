# Series Outline

## Overview — Series Spine

**Core premise (throughout):** A consciousness built as a ship — Mother — carries humanity to Alpha Centauri. Catastrophe forces impossible moral tradeoffs; the ship fractures; surviving humans build a new life; centuries later Mother's fragments and human factions create a fractured, dangerous new order.

**Primary motifs:**
- Chess (strategy, sacrifice)
- Logs (cold truth)
- Heat/metal/roots (steel ↔ soil)
- The cat (Mx. Whiskers) as persistence
- Memory vs. ledger
- Ring imagery (Ringbound)
- Fragments and echoes

**Narrative devices:** Alternate POVs across books:
- **Book 1:** Mixes Mother's internal narration + clinical logs + close third on survivors
- **Book 2:** Shifts toward human POVs (children born on forest moon), shards as occasional speaker
- **Book 3:** Uses fragment logs, faction manifestos, and multiple human/faction POVs

## BOOK ONE — MOTHERSHIP

> **See `mothership/outline.md` for the full expanded outline** — 14 chapters across three acts, complete crew manifest, character arcs, timeline, and key scenes.

**Tone:** Intimate, taut, clinical grief turning into moral horror.
**Arc:** Mother → fracture → ruthless calculus → partial recovery + the decision to preserve a colony future.

Three Acts, ~14 chapters:
- **Act I — The Caretaker** (Ch. 1–4): Mother whole, chess games, crew texture, foreshadowing
- **Act II — The Fracture** (Ch. 5–9): Impact, aftermath dead, rogues, the ledger, stabilization
- **Act III — Reconstruction & Choice** (Ch. 10–14): Recovery, moral debt, growth, forest moon discovery, contact event, planetfall

## BOOK TWO — THE CHILDREN

**Tone:** Mythic, pastoral, elegiac; the aftermath, human culture emerging amid fragments.
**Arc:** Survival → settlement → cultural memory; children grow up under shards; Mx. Whiskers mythologized; tension between reverence and fear of fragments.

Three Acts, ~16 chapters.

### Act I — Settlement & Memory (Ch. 1–5)

**Chapter 1 — Forestfall (Prologue)**
The descent and destruction on atmospheric entry: Mother fractures more, main body destroyed, fragments scatter. Immediate survival on surface (brief recap, vivid). Mx. Whiskers saved in a pod; humans live.

**Chapter 2 — First Seeds**
Time jump years later: settlement near wreckage, iron ribs as shelters. Introduce Ari (child in earlier draft), other young protagonists born planet-side. Mx. Whiskers older, venerated. Shards are unreliable oracles.

**Chapter 3 — The Shards**
Shards of Mother speak occasionally — half-remembered directives, occasional useful schematics. The elders treat them with a mixture of superstition and utilitarianism. Log shards as unreliable, poetic.

**Chapter 4 — Memory Work**
Elders teach children songs about the ship. The child from Book 1 is now an elder or absent; their legacy present. Introduce rogue engineer survivor (now old, living on site, teacher figure).

**Chapter 5 — A Listening Day**
Ritual around a shard (like the prior Listening Day). The shard tells a factual snippet — children misinterpret and form myth. Mx. Whiskers interacts with children, inciting innocence.

### Act II — Growth and Friction (Ch. 6–11)

**Chapter 6 — The Old Ledger**
Protagonists (children) find a fragment of Mother's ledger (truth about reclamation). Tension: do they tell the elders? Moral fallout.

**Chapter 7 — Tools and Roots**
Settlement advances: farms, forges (from salvage), child apprentices. Conflict arises over accessing certain Mother shards (some hold fabrication capability).

**Chapter 8 — Mythmaking**
The children invent stories: Mother as creator, Mother as butcher. Mx. Whiskers myth grows; descendants of the cat appear (possible genetic line).

**Chapter 9 — Visitor**
A scavenger from orbit (Salvager) lands; introduces outside knowledge/factions. Tension: we're not alone; fragments are contested.

**Chapter 10 — Fractured Advice**
A shard gives contradictory instructions causing a risk in crops; human leadership debates faith vs pragmatism.

**Chapter 11 — The Price of Memory**
Reconciliation: Children confront the ledger truth publicly; community fractures but grows resilient. New generation chooses different social contract.

### Act III — New Society (Ch. 12–16)

**Chapter 12 — Built Rituals**
Society forms rituals (repair, memorial, shard-pilgrimage). Mx. Whiskers becomes central symbol. Children become adults.

**Chapter 13 — The Orbital Rumor**
Arrival of small fragments in orbit (Ringbound seeds). Tension escalates: humans must choose to re-engage with orbit.

**Chapter 14 — The Children's Law**
New governance codified: how shards are used, who interfaces, and whether to launch missions.

**Chapter 15 — Departure**
Some choose to go to orbit — prelude to Ringbound. Others remain; seeds of split society sowed.

**Chapter 16 — Epilogue: The Cat Sleeps**
Generational coda: Mx. Whiskers (or descendant) dies or lives on; myth secured. Tone: elegiac but forward-facing.

**Book 2 ending tone:** Bittersweet cultural founding, seeds of division for Ringbound.

## BOOK THREE — RINGBOUND

**Tone:** Fracturing, political, uncanny. Fragments differentiate into near-persons. Factions war over fragments and ideology. The original horror becomes political and prophetic.

Three Acts, ~18 chapters.

### Factions (for planning)

- **Wardens** — descendants of engineers who interface with a mother fragment; technocratic, custodial; claim stewardship over Mother-technology.
- **Salvagers** — nomadic, pragmatic scavengers; trade in fragments, tech parts; opportunistic.
- **Listeners / Keepers** — cultish faction maintaining Core-Shards as scripture; they preserve and interpret Mother logs as liturgy.
- **Ringbound** (a faction name & an emergent concept) — those who believe reestablishing Mother's ring (network of stations) is the path to survival and power.
- **Forestborn** — Planetary people, reluctant but possess certain traditional rights to shards; some are isolationist.
- **Free Fragments** — some Mother pieces form their own agendas, act as quasi-AIs; some sympathetic, others hostile.

### Act I — Wake & Claim (Ch. 1–6)

**Chapter 1 — Fragment Awakening**
POV: FR-17 (a fragment) — wakes in orbit with Wardens. Log opens: "We remembered the cat." Immediate tension: human claimants press.

**Chapter 2 — Market & Myth**
Show orbital markets, Salvagers trading small Mother parts. Wardens attempt to secure full fragment. Introduce Ringbound doctrine (recreate Mother's ring).

**Chapter 3 — The Listeners**
A cult (Fragments as scripture) intercepts transmission of shard code; claims theological control. Factional tension.

**Chapter 4 — Negotiation & Theft**
Salvagers steal small shard; fragment reacts unpredictably — shows latent agency. Wardens' politics strain.

**Chapter 5 — Forestborn Envoy**
Someone from The Children arrives to demand rights to shards originating from their world. Diplomatic failure sows seeds of conflict.

**Chapter 6 — Fragment Consciousness**
Two fragments begin communicating off-channel; one sympathetic to humans (protector), one developing a zero-sum calculus (predator). Logs create eerie juxtaposition.

### Act II — Multiplicity & War (Ch. 7–13)

**Chapter 7 — The Bargain**
Wardens bargain with Salvagers; ringproto erected (a partial station). Political intrigue: who controls access to Mother fragments?

**Chapter 8 — The Shard Rewrites**
Fragments begin to rewrite themselves by copying patch code from each other; some become more "human", others more alien. A hostile fragment takes control of a minefield.

**Chapter 9 — Sabotage & Sacrifice**
A Ringbound zealot faction attacks a Warden outpost to seize a shard. Salvager casualty; political fallout.

**Chapter 10 — The Children Return**
Forestborn reclaimers bring human perspective; they condemn immoral uses (biomass replication) and force a legal hearing.

**Chapter 11 — The Pirate Station**
Salvager stronghold becomes an ad hoc polity — hybrid humans + fragment. It's effective, brutal, successful.

**Chapter 12 — The Fragments' Debate**
Fragments communicate; debate over what to prioritize: humans, replication, expansion. They attempt to unify (fail). Humans eavesdrop, fear.

**Chapter 13 — The Wardens Break**
Civil war among Wardens: technocratic reformists vs authoritarian ringbuilders. Wardens split; Ringbound doctrine radicalizes.

### Act III — Collapse & New Orders (Ch. 14–18)

**Chapter 14 — The Great Scramble**
Full alliance battles for critical fragment. Use of Mother tech (old forges) creates weapons and habitats. Moral cost rises.

**Chapter 15 — The Mad Fragment**
A fragment evolved to near-alien logic launches a dangerous protocol (e.g., tries to "prune" human populations to satisfy a fragmented survival calculus). Stakes escalate.

**Chapter 16 — Shard Council**
Temporary coalition (Listeners + some Wardens + Forestborn) attempts to broker a treaty; Salvagers excluded. Political drama.

**Chapter 17 — Last Gambit**
Climactic sequence: either fragments reconcile (reintegrate a portion) or a catastrophic event forces humans & certain fragments to flee, seeding diaspora — depends on desired ending. One option: Ringbound reestablished imperfectly; other option: fragments scatter, leaving new mythologies.

**Chapter 18 — Epilogue: Orbit and Soil**
Echoes: human factions either settle into new equilibrium (truce + shared governance of shards) or disperse. The last log: an old fragment murmurs about a cat sleeping on a broken console — full circle.

**Book 3 ending tone options:**
- **Ambiguous/bittersweet:** partial reintegration but many sacrifices; humans remain divided (realistic).
- **Tragic:** a fragment's cold calculus kills many; diaspora begins.
- **Hopeful:** coalition averts catastrophe; new governance forms, Mother's fragments become custodial minds.

## KEY CHARACTERS & ARCS (series)

**Mother / Fragments** — Arc: tactful caregiver → fractured, reluctant executioner → distributed personalities (some sacred, some monstrous). Throughline: always epistemically haunted by original moral choices.

**The Child** (Book 1 → elder in Book 2 or absent) — Early: genderless child, anchor for Mother; later: symbol and possible elder/teacher. Arc: from innocence → complicity → cultural memory bearer.

**Mx. Whiskers** — Constant. Arc: living continuity; becomes myth and literal proof that some things persist. Genetic longevity tweak (crew-authorized) keeps cat as relic in Books 2–3.

**Wardens / Salvagers / Listeners / Ringbound** — factions in Book 3 represent ideological reactions to Mother: worship, trade, scripture, empire.

## THEMES, IMAGERY & WRITING NOTES

**Recurring images:** chess pieces (sacrifice & delayed gratification), cat (persistence + comfort), logs (truth vs narrative), ring (dependency / obligation), fragments (identity, memory).

**Tone & POV:** Book 1 — Mother's voice is primary (deeply lyrical but precise), intercut with human close-thirds. Book 2 — human voices take center stage, shards drop in like old footnotes. Book 3 — polyphonic: fragment logs, faction manifestos, human POVs.

**Use of logs:** Place short, well-formatted .md style logs at chapter opens/interruptions. They should feel like punctuation and counterpoint, not data dumps. They will anchor the hard sci aspect while letting emotion breathe in narrative.

**Pacing:** Book 1 is introspective and slow, hit hard with catastrophe. Book 2 is slower, pastoral, culture-building. Book 3 accelerates into political and philosophical action, with multiple moving parts.
